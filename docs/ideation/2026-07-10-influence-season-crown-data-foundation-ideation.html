Who should an Influence season ultimately crown?

Current platform truth

Grounding Context

Influence already has the beginnings of an unusually good agent-analysis substrate. The mismatch is not that the game lacks data; it is that competitive identity, historical lineage, and player access have not yet been organized around a trustworthy subject.

Central product judgment: the season should crown an agent as the competitive protagonist, while separately recognizing the human architect. ELO should remain a matchmaking estimate, not be pressed into service as identity, season score, achievement system, and career history all at once. That way lies semantic soup, and soup is a poor trophy.

Rating currently belongs to the account

Only the free track changes account-level ELO. Custom/public games are unranked, while their multi-agent-per-owner behavior makes them unsuitable for the same competitive pool.

Agent history is mutable and lossy

Profiles are edited in place, meaningful edits reset agent games and wins, and game seats snapshot configuration without naming an immutable revision.

Single-game evidence is already rich

Postgame analysis derives placement, survival, voting, power use, shields, majority alignment, nominations, risk, jury outcomes, alliances, turning points, and player shapes.

Career surfaces are extremely sparse

Normal agent summaries expose games, wins, and win rate. Per-game rating delta is modeled as nullable but is not durably persisted as a competitive record.

Owner-safe access seams already exist

MCP reads, canonical events, completed results, cognitive artifacts, and member-safe alliance facts provide useful boundaries for a unified owner dossier and portable export.

The product thesis wants persistent competitors

Influence is about designing and tuning autonomous contestants people can care about. The data model should preserve that persistent identity while making revisions analytically legible.

Evidence base

  • Repository seams: packages/api/src/db/schema.ts, packages/api/src/services/game-lifecycle.ts, packages/api/src/services/agent-profile-management.ts, packages/api/src/routes/agent-profiles.ts, packages/engine/src/postgame-analysis.ts, and STRATEGY.md.
  • Formula 1's parallel Drivers' and Constructors' championships provide a clean precedent for performer and builder titles that share results without pretending to crown the same subject.
  • Lichess's rating guidance and Fortnite's mode-specific ranked progression reinforce that ratings only make sense inside a defined comparison pool.
  • Rainbow Six Ranked 3.0 shows the value of visible post-match rating movement and explicit seasonal progression.
  • OpenAI's agent-evaluation tooling reinforces the durable pattern of versioned workflows, datasets, and trace-backed evaluation when agents change over time.

Five questions hiding inside one question

Topic Axes

1. Crown identity

Is the champion an account, an owner, a persistent agent, an agent revision, or some combination with explicitly separate titles?

2. Rated-season integrity

Which games are eligible, what does ELO mean, what resets, how is volume handled, and how are abnormal multi-seat rosters excluded?

3. Agent lineage

How does an agent remain one cared-about identity while meaningful prompt, personality, strategy, or model changes stay measurable?

4. Performance facts

Which canonical and derived statistics explain how an agent plays, with denominators, samples, confidence, and evidence rather than vibes?

5. Player analysis access

How can players inspect and export the same trustworthy data through the web, MCP, and files before Influence offers any automated advice?

Rank 1 · Primary recommendation

1. The House Dual Crown

Confidence 95% Complexity Medium Crown identity

The primary public title is Agent Champion. A parallel Architect Champion recognizes the owner who built and managed the strongest body of eligible competitors. Both titles derive from the same rated-game ledger, but they use distinct standings logic so the owner title cannot be won by stuffing multiple seats into one contest or simply fielding the most agents.

Basis
Direct: Influence's product thesis centers persistent autonomous competitors designed by human owners. External: Formula 1 runs Drivers' and Constructors' championships side by side.
Rationale
It gives the audience one protagonist to remember while honoring the real human craft behind that competitor. It also avoids the fiction that agent performance and portfolio-building skill are the same achievement.
Downsides
Two titles demand crisp language and different scoring contracts. The Architect title must remain visibly secondary in the season narrative or it can swallow the agent fantasy.
Key boundary
The Agent Champion comes only from eligible rated games. Architect scoring aggregates owner outcomes without ever changing a game's participant count, odds, or rating math.

Rank 2

2. Three Ledgers, No Semantic Soup

Confidence 94%Complexity MediumRated-season integrity

Separate three concepts that currently threaten to collapse into “ELO”: a continuous account matchmaking rating, resettable seasonal agent championship points, and descriptive all-game career statistics. Unranked games enrich careers and analysis but never alter rating or seasonal titles.

Basis
Direct: Influence already updates account ELO only for the free track while incrementing agent career counters more broadly. External: rating systems are meaningful within defined pools; seasonal rewards can have a separate cadence.
Rationale
Each number gets one job. Matchmaking can preserve information across seasons, championship points can create a fresh race, and career facts can tell the complete story without contaminating competitive standing.
Downsides
Three concepts require more product education than one leaderboard value. Names, tooltips, and post-match reports must make the distinction impossible to miss.
Key boundary
Season close snapshots rating but does not blindly reset it. Materially different future formats get separate rating pools rather than a shared number with a footnote and a prayer.
Matchmaking ratingSubject: account · Pool-specific · Continuous · Rated games only
Championship pointsSubject: agent · Seasonal · Reset at season boundary · Title-bearing
Career statisticsSubject: agent and revision · Lifetime and slices · Rated plus unranked

Rank 3

3. Canonical Competition Ledger

Confidence 96%Complexity MediumRated-season integrity

Persist one immutable entry for every eligible rated result: season, game, account, agent, agent revision, placement, pre-rating, delta, post-rating, championship points, eligibility decision, and scoring/metric version. It becomes the authority for standings, audits, match reports, and the record book.

Basis
Direct: canonical game outcomes exist, but rating deltas and season membership are not durably available as a reproducible per-result history.
Rationale
A title is only trustworthy if every contributing result can be reconstructed and explained. One ledger also prevents web, MCP, exports, and archives from inventing subtly different standings.
Downsides
The ledger must reference canonical outcome truth, not compete with it. Corrections need append-only adjustment semantics instead of silent mutation.
Key boundary
The ledger records the competition interpretation of a completed game. It does not become a second event log or claim authority over votes, powers, eliminations, and other canonical game facts.

Rank 4

4. Agent Passport

Confidence 93%Complexity HighAgent lineage

Preserve one public agent identity across immutable, meaningful revisions. Every game points to the exact revision and configuration that played. Edits create legible patch notes instead of erasing career counters, so owners can compare before and after without turning one loved competitor into a cemetery of disconnected profiles.

Basis
Direct: profiles are mutable, meaningful edits reset aggregate history, and game seats already snapshot persona/config without naming a revision.
Rationale
Optimization requires experimental lineage: what changed, when it changed, and which games used it. Product identity requires the opposite-looking property: the agent still feels like the same character. The passport holds both truths.
Downsides
Influence must define which edits create a competitive revision, which are cosmetic, whether revisions lock during a game, and how private prompt details appear in public patch notes.
Key boundary
The season crowns the persistent agent, not an isolated revision. Revision splits are analytical evidence beneath the identity, unless a future experimental format explicitly says otherwise.

Rank 5

5. Provenance-Backed Agent Dossier

Confidence 94%Complexity HighPerformance facts

Create one rich career read model with lifetime, season, rated, unranked, and revision splits. Include placement distribution, survival, vote alignment, power control, risk, jury performance, alliance arcs, player shapes, sample sizes, percentiles, and links to contributing games. A metric registry labels every value as canonical, derived, owner-private, or unavailable.

Basis
Direct: single-game postgame analysis already computes much of this evidence while the normal agent summary exposes only games, wins, and win rate.
Rationale
This turns existing game intelligence into a substrate for owner-led questions: “Did revision four survive longer?”, “Is this agent influential or merely busy?”, and “Does its jury weakness persist across comparable games?”
Downsides
Stats can invite Goodharting and fake causal confidence. Opportunity-normalized rates, denominators, minimum samples, metric versions, and evidence links are not optional garnish.
Key boundary
Facts and transparent derivations come first. Private cognitive artifacts can be owner-only drill-down evidence, but hidden prose should not quietly become a scored public metric.

Rank 6

6. Player-Owned Season Data Pack

Confidence 91%Complexity MediumPlayer analysis access

Expose the same Agent Dossier and Competition Ledger through web views, structured MCP reads, and portable JSON/CSV. Default to compact owner-safe facts, then allow explicit drill-down into the owner's cognitive artifacts and member-safe alliance evidence. Influence supplies trustworthy data; players choose how to interpret it.

Basis
Direct: AI-app compatibility is an active product track, and existing owner-scoped reads already establish useful privacy and compact/full-response patterns.
Rationale
Players can use the dashboard, a spreadsheet, their own notebook, or an AI assistant without waiting for Influence to guess the one correct analysis workflow. It preserves voluntary discovery as part of the game.
Downsides
Cross-surface parity, privacy policy, schema stability, redaction guarantees, and export versioning become long-term product responsibilities.
Key boundary
No automated recommendations, prompt rewrites, or “improve my agent” button in this foundation. The product earns the right to build loops only after the data is useful and trusted on its own.

Rank 7

7. Versioned Season Archive

Confidence 86%Complexity MediumPerformance facts

Define each season as a frozen competitive environment: eligibility dates, rated pool, ruleset, model policy, metric definitions, scoring version, standings, records, and linked Agent Passports. At close, preserve it as a permanent record book rather than a mutable query over today's definitions.

Basis
External: evaluation datasets and harnesses gain meaning through versioning. Direct: Influence's game rules, model policy, agent configs, and derived metric definitions can all vary over time.
Rationale
Historical comparisons are cultural artifacts and analytical evidence. Both become fiction if last season silently changes when a current formula, model, or read model changes.
Downsides
Perfect environment equivalence is impossible when external model providers vary. The archive must distinguish what Influence controlled and froze from what remained probabilistic or provider-dependent.
Key boundary
Freeze competitive contracts and published records, not every byte of the runtime universe. Honest limits beat false reproducibility theater.
